Chapter 2

The Discovery

In university, I took the introductory Python course. It clicked immediately: programming just made sense to me. We had a brief section about data analysis, and I found it absolutely fascinating. Entire worlds of knowledge, hidden in a mountain of data.

From my personal experience playing the video game Overwatch, I knew about the E-Sports League stats lab and talked to my professor about it. We designed a small task together for the rest of the course, and it was still part of this material when I left university. I've played Overwatch since its release, sometimes competitively (top 1.5% of the world), so I had deep domain knowledge about what mattered.

Once I got around to playing with the actual data, I loved being able to show stuff like "the amount of healing doesn't matter statistically at all in this team composition, but matters a lot in this one." Feelings you had as a player, proven with science. Seeing these worlds through the noise is incredibly fun for me.

Chapter 3

Learning Through Failure & Success

After that first project, I wanted to try something more ambitious. Fascinated by extreme weather and environmental events, I saw a chance to learn geospatial analysis applied to the 2021 La Palma volcanic eruption: something completely new to me.

I started with raw Sentinel satellite imagery and taught myself rasterio workflows from scratch. The trickiest part was integrating DEM data: different formats, mismatched resolutions, alignment issues. Building that reprojection workflow piece by piece took a while, but finally seeing vegetation indices plotted in 3D alongside elevation was a breakthrough moment.

Not everything worked though. In a different project, I wanted to track biodiversity using GBIF data and relate it to climate variables. I never managed to get properly normalized information because I couldn't figure out how to adjust for the growing number of contributions and the bias that, quite simply, you need humans to be somewhere to see an animal and report it. Sometimes projects don't work, but luckily that did not stop me.

Chapter 4

Toward Research

I've long been fascinated by the idea of working at a research institute, contributing to our understanding of the natural world. The Alfred Wegener Institute in Germany is a particular inspiration. I was even accepted for an internship with their data science support group, though I couldn't attend due to scheduling. That conversation confirmed this path is possible and worth preparing for.

So I started working on Arctic sea ice extent forecasting. The project combines NSIDC sea ice data with ERA5 climate reanalysis in PostGIS, building LSTM models for daily, weekly, and monthly predictions with tiered spatial aggregation and a Parquet feature store.

My path isn't traditional. I started studying engineering, dropped out, and I'm now doing an apprenticeship as a Fachinformatiker. My credentials aren't what most institutions look for. But I'm confident I can learn anything I put my mind to - I've done so before. This work is my way of building toward opportunities in climate and earth science research.
